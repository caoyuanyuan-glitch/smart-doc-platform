# MGI英文技术文档写作风格指南

This guide provides writing rules for MGI technical writers, editors, reviewers, and UI designers\. These guidelines help to ensure that the MGI technical information is consistent, accurate, and easy to understand\.

# Language

## Tense

Use present tense in most cases\. Do not use past tense, and use future tense only when necessary\.

User documentation tells the users how to perform tasks, and the tasks take place in the present\. Besides, present tense is easier to read and understand\.

|**MGI style**|**Non\-MGI style**|
|---|---|
|After you power on the device, self\-test begins\.|After you power on the device, the self\- test will begin\.|
|If you open the door, the sequencing run stops\.|If you open the door, the sequencing run will be stopped\.|

## Voice

Use active voice in most cases\. Active voice is direct, short, and clear to provide information\.

Rewrite the sentences if *to be*, *will be *are overused\.

Use passive voice only when the action performer is unknown, unimportant, or when the active voice might convey a sense of blaming or frustration to the user\.

|---|---|
|You can prepare libraries manually or use the third\-party systems\.|The library can be prepared manually or using third\- party systems\.|
|In the wash interface, tap Wash\. A confirmation dialogue box is displayed\.||

## Tone

Tones express your attitude to the subject and the audience\. Use the following tones in your technical writing:

###### Helpful

Concrete and accurate information establishes a helpful tone\.

###### Direct

Writing clear information establishes a direct tone\.

###### Confident

Providing information that is certain, positively, and professionally establishes a confident tone\.

- Do not omit definite article before attributive clauses\. Excluding them makes reading and translation more difficult\.

- Use *the *when a particular noun has already been mentioned previously\.

- Use *the *to talk about a specific object known to the reader\.

## Abbreviations and acronyms

An abbreviation is a shortened form of a written word or phrase\. Abbreviations may be used to save space and time, to avoid repetition of long words and phrases, or simply to conform to conventional usage\.

Acronym is a type of abbreviation\. An acronym is a word formed from the initial letter or letters of each of the successive parts or major parts of a compound term\.

We recommend that you provide a list of abbreviations and acronyms in the technical documents for better understanding\.

1. Include technical terms, acronyms, and abbreviations in the glossary\.

2. Use parentheses to enclose the abbreviation or acronym the first time it is mentioned\.

3. Use abbreviations when:

    1. space is limited, for example, in a table or figure\.

    2. abbreviations are well known to readers, for example, UPS\.

    3. meaning of abbreviations is clear and easy to understand\.

4. Avoid abbreviations when:

    1. they seldom occur in the content, for example, only one, or two times\.

    2. meaning of abbreviations is ambiguous and hard to understand\.

5. Choose *a/an *according to how an abbreviation is spoken\.

6. Add a lowercase *s *for the plural form of abbreviation\.

7. Do not use abbreviations as verbs, use them as nouns or adjectives\.

8. Do not use *‘s *to show the possessive form of an abbreviation\.

9. Do not use plural form of an abbreviation for a unit of measurement\.

10. Do not use Latin abbreviations\. In general, Latin abbreviations are commonly used\. However, in formal and technical writing, their use is discouraged because if the document is localized, Latin abbreviations may not be recognized by or easily translated into some other languages\.

## Capitalization

- Use initial capitalization for the first letter of the first word for headings, figure/table captions, interface labels, and general texts \(sentence style\)\.

- Ensure that the capitalization is consistent\. Inconsistent capitalization can cause extra translation costs and can confuse users\.

- Use capitalization for proper nouns\.

- Proper nouns refer to specific persons, places, and things\. Proper nouns include:

    - Trademarks

    - Product names

    - Specific groups/ organizations /places

    - Name/title of a person

    - Titles of books

- Do not use capitalization for common nouns\. Common nouns refer to a class of entities, or non\-unique instances of a specific class\. Translators might think the common noun as a proper noun and therefore do not translate it\.

- Match the capitalization and the exact words that are displayed in the interface\.

- Capitalize the first letter of the first word of each item in a vertical list/table\.

- Confirm the capitalization for the spelled\-out form of an abbreviation; abbreviations do not mean you need to capitalize every word\.

- Do not hide the verb in a passive sentence, or use nominalization, or use complex and longer variations of verbs\.

## Lists

- Ensure that a list contains at least two list items\.

- Use ordered list for items/steps that are sequential\.

- Use unordered list for items/steps that are not sequential\.

- Use an imperative sentence to start each step\.

- In an unordered list, arrange list items in a logical way\. If there is no better method for arranging them, put them in alphabetical order\.

- Write a complete leading sentence to start the list, and end the sentence with a colon\.

- Capitalize the first word of each list item\.

- Do not add punctuation after each list item, except for complete sentences\.

- Use grammatically parallel style for each list item to make the list easy to scan\.

# Punctuation

### Brackets and angle brackets

- Avoid using brackets \(\[ \]\), except for enclosing editorial interpolations, explanations, translations of foreign terms, or corrections\.

- Use right angle brackets to abbreviate simple sequences\. Add a space before and after each bracket, and do not use boldface for brackets\.

- Angle brackets can be used to enclose tags in certain markup languages, for example, XML\.

### Exclamation points and question marks

- Avoid using exclamation points and question marks in most technical writing, except for error messages or on\-screen instructions\.

- Do not use exclamation points or question marks for indirect speech\.

### Parentheses

- Avoid placing important things in parentheses in running text, because parentheses can interrupt the flow of a sentence, and might easily be ignored\.

- Use a period inside parentheses when a parenthetical content is a complete sentence\.

- Use a period outside parentheses when only a part of a sentence is in the parentheses and that part cannot stand alone grammatically\.

- Use parentheses for abbreviations, symbols, and measurements\.

# Structure

This chapter helps you to present information in a clear and logical structure\.

## Topic\-based writing

For technical communicators *topic *refers to a small independent piece of information on a single subject\.

A topic has the following characteristics:

- It is meaningful when it is displayed alone\.

- It follows the rules for a specific topic type\.

- Topic\-based writing is an approach to writing well\-structured, minimalist information that is usually presented online\.

- There are three main topic types:

- Task

- Concept

- Reference

## Task topics

Task topics document the steps that users must complete to accomplish a task or goal\.

Task topics provide procedures, typically in step\-by\-step instructions\. Some task topics might list choices as bulleted points rather than steps, or they might describe a single action rather than a sequence of steps\. Task topics also provide information about the context \(where to perform a task and when\), the rationale \(why to perform a task\), prerequisites, and examples\.

As you write task information, note what concepts need to be documented, and to what depth, for users to complete the tasks successfully\. Consider the probable skills and experience of users, and write with those characteristics in mind\. For example, if users must configure TCP/IP and might not know about routers, create and link to a concept topic about routers\.

The goal of minimalist writing is to reduce the amount of reading that users must do before they can successfully complete a task\.

### Headings of task topics

Task topic headings must be precise, distinct, and clearly convey what the task is\.

Follow these guidelines when you write task topic headings:

- Start the heading with a gerund phrase \(a phrase that starts with an \-ing verb\)\.

    Example:

    Prepare the laboratory \(×\)

    Preparing the laboratory \(√\) Inspect the device \(×\) Inspecting the device \(√\)

- Describe the user’s goal, not the tool that is used to perform the task\.

    Example:

    Using the device \(×\)

    Turning on/off the device \(√\)

- Make the topic heading specific enough so that a user who sees it in a list of search results knows exactly what the topic contains\.

    Example:

    Preparing necessities \(×\)

    Preparing a sample library and reagent kit \(√\)

### Structure of task topics

The basic structure of a task topic includes a brief introduction, a prerequisite and steps\. However, its structure can vary depending on the task\. You can include conceptual or reference information in a task and so on\.

### Introductions for task topics

Start a task topic with an introductory sentence or paragraph that specifies the purpose or rationale for performing the task\. This introductory sentence can provide the additional information that the title is not fully conveyed\.

### Prerequisites for task topics

A prerequisite is any requirement that must be met, any task that the user must perform, or any authority or privilege that must be set up before the user can start the current task\. Not all tasks have prerequisites\. The prerequisites must be listed before the first step of the task\.

### Steps in task topics

Use numbered lists to describe tasks if there is more than one sequential step\. Try to limit the number of steps to nine\. And keep descriptions of each task as short as possible\.

## Concept topics

Concept topics explain ideas that the user must understand to complete a task\. Every concept topic performs at least one of the following functions:

- Introduces a solution, process, product, tool, or feature

- Provides background information and explains issues that users must know before working with a system or component or before starting a task

- Describes how one feature, tool, or product is related to others and how they work together or do not work together

- Describes any restrictions that limit the circumstances in which a tool can be used successfully

### Headings of concept topics

- The heading for a concept topic must be a specific, meaningful noun or noun phrase\.

- Follow these guidelines when you write concept topic headings:

- Place important words at the beginning of a heading to focus attention on those words\.

    Example:

    Engineer GUI software Interface overview \(×\)

    Interface overview of Engineer GUI software \(√\)

- Do not use a gerund for a concept heading because gerunds indicate tasks\.

    Example:

    Engineer GUI software Interface overview \(×\)

    Interface overview of Engineer GUI software \(√\)

### Structure of concept topics

Concept topics should be written in paragraphs and a bulleted list\. A concept topic must address only one complete idea\.

Standards to determine an effective concept topic:

- The heading is a noun or phrase\.

- The introduction \(short description\) explains why the concept is important to understand\.

- The structure consists of paragraphs and unordered lists\.

- A definition is used to introduce a new concept or related concept\.

- The topic is relatively short but conveys the necessary information\. Example of a concept topic:

- Negative pressure system

- The negative pressure system is used to provide vacuum pressure, adsorb the flowcell, and ensure that no relative motion of the flowcell when the XY stage starts, accelerates or decelerates\. The vacuum pump is controlled by the I/O board\.

- Negative pressure system includes:

    - Vacuum pump: used for providing negative pressure\.

    - Muffler: used for reducing noises caused by vacuum pump\.

    - Digital pressure switch: used for detecting vacuum pressure, displaying pressure values, and providing status data on the user interface\.

    - Air filter: used for filtering impurities from the air extracted by the flowcell platform to avoid damage to the digital pressure switch and vacuum pump\.

    - Flowcell adsorption button: press to activate the vacuum pump\.

    - Flowcell platform: used for firmly adsorbing the bottom of the flowcell\. This is because, during the operation of the vacuum pump, a vacuum environment is formed by the vacuum pipelines and the closed grooves on the flowcell platform\. The flowcell platform is a precision metal platform with closed grooves and holes to connect the vacuum pipeline\.

## Reference topics

Reference topics provide quick access to information that users need to complete tasks\. Make reference topics easy to scan because users typically look for reference material only when they need it, rather than studying and learning it as they do with concepts\.

### Headings of reference topics

The headings for reference topics are typically nouns or noun strings\.

Follow these guidelines when you write reference topic headings:

- Do not begin a heading with an article \(*a*, *an*, or *the*\)\.

- Title the topics for a particular category of reference information consistently\.

### Structure of reference topics

Reference topics often include subsections to help users locate particular information\. When the topic heading requires explanation, provide introductory information to help users quickly determine whether they want to read more\.

# Numbers and measurements

This chapter helps you to express numerical information\.

## Numerals vs words

In general, if the number is less than 10, use word numbers \(such as one, two\)\. If the number is larger than or equal to 10, use numerals \(such as 10, 11\)\.

## Range

When expressing ranges of numbers, follow the following guidelines:

- Use the word *to *with a space on each side of the word to indicate a range of numbers, because the hyphen could be misinterpreted as a minus sign\.

    Example:

    Temperature: \-20 ℃ to 50 ℃ \(\-4 ℉ to 122 ℉ \)

    Relative humidity: 20% RH to 80% RH, no condensing

- If the range includes a unit of measurement, repeat the unit on both sides of the hyphen for a better understanding\.

    Example:

    Voltage: 100 V \- 240 V\~

- Always use numerals in ranges\.

- Do not use words such as *between *and *and *to show a range\. Because the user would be confused whether the two numbers are the boundaries of the range or are included in the range\.

    Example:

    Enter a value between 32,000 and 485,000\. \(×\)

    Enter a value in the range 32000 \- 485000\. \(√\)

- Do not use words such as *from *and *between *with a hyphenated range\.

    Example:

    Choose a number from 1 \- 20\. \(×\)

    Choose a number from 1 to 20\. \(√\)

- Use *greater than or equal to *or *less than or equal to *to express the symbol ≥ or ≤ when writing a complete sentence\. Use the symbol ≥ or ≤ or use a heading such as *maximum requirement *or *minimum requirement *when writing a phrase\.

    Example:

    The required power supply of the UPS should be equal or greater than 12 kVA\.

    Altitude: ≤ 3000 m \(9843 ft\)

    Maximum altitude: 3000 m \(9843 ft\)

## Percentage

Use numerals and the percent sign \(%\) rather than the word percent\. Do not leave a space between the numeral and the percent sign\. However, if the percentage is a cardinal number and is at the beginning of a sentence, spell out both the number and the word percent\.

## Rounding numbers

When you convert to a different unit of measurement, express the result with a similar precision to the original measurement\.

**Example:**

The laboratory ground should be level, with a similar precision to the original measurement\.

**Example:**

The laboratory ground should be level, with a gradient of less than 1/200 and the laboratory table can bear a weight of over 200 kg \(441 lb\)\.

### Error messages

Error messages are used to inform users that a problem in the system or application occurred\.

Messages that address complicated error conditions should include the following components:

- The message text, which succinctly yet completely describes the problem\.

- Explanatory text, which provides a detailed explanation of the problem\.

- If applicable, information about the state of the system or product environment as a result of the problem\.

- An action or actions that users can take to remedy the problem\. 

Error messages can be short phases or sentences\.

#### Short phase

Follow the following guidelines:

- Use sentence\-style capitalization, capitalize only the first element in the word unless a subsequent element is a proper noun or proper adjective\.

    Example:

    Auto focus error

    Location error

- Use the phase structures of noun \+ noun or noun \+ adjective\.

- Do not add full stop at the end of the phase\.

- If you need to abbreviate the word due to length limit, use the common abbreviation\. Do not create new words by yourself\. You can check the abbreviation at [*https://www\.abbreviations\.com*](https://www.abbreviations.com/)\.

    Example:

    Temp\. control error

    Auto focus init\. error

#### Sentence

Follow the following guidelines:

- Use sentence\-style capitalization and write the sentence completely and grammatically\. The sentence should end with a full stop\.

    Example:

    - New curve takes effect after submit\. \(×\)

    - New curve takes effect after clicking **Submit**\. \(√\)

- Do not make the reader feel stupid or at fault, even if the problem is a result of user error\. It is all right to use the passive voice to describe the error condition\.

    Example:

    - You have entered the same library ID\. \(×\)

    - Different library ID is required\. \(√\)

- Write most error messages in third person unless you are asking for a response or giving an instruction\.

    Example:

    Clean is completed, are you sure to stop?

- Avoid *please*\. It can be interpreted to mean that a required action is optional, except *please wait*\.

    Example:

    - Startup failed, please restart the device\. \(×\)

    - Startup failed, restart the device\. \(√\)

- Avoid uppercase text and exclamation points\.

    Example:

    - Close the DOOR\! \(×\)

    - Close the door\. \(√\)

- Avoid phrasing that will seem silly to the user, such as *unexpected error*\.

- Write the error messages from the user’s point of view\.

    Example:

    Required Value

    You must specify a value for each required field\. \(×\) 

    Specify a network name

    Tap **Settings \> Wifi **to choose a network to connect to\. \(√\)

- Avoid using jargon unless it’s appropriate for your audience and no other word fits the situation\.

    Example:

    Error: The protocol has changed to a disabled locked network\. \(×\)

    Error: You have changed to an insecure network\. \(√\)

    ### Keyboard and mouse controls

- Write the name of the key as it is written on a standard keyboard\.

- When instructing users to press multiple keys, use “\+” to connect the key names\. Do not leave a space before and after “\+”\.

    Example:

    Press **Ctrl\+Alt\+Delete**\.

- For CG technical information, use *select *instead of *click *or *tap *when the user needs to click a button/icon once or click to select a choice\.

### Commands, parameters, and options in running text

- In running text, use a monospaced font for a command\.

- When you refer to a command, parameter, or option in running text, specify the word *command *, *parameter *, or *option *after the name\. Command, parameter, and option names are not translated, so without the identifying noun, users might be confused about what the name represents\.

    Example:

    Execute the mdadm \-D command to check the array state\. \(√\)

- Do not use a command name as a verb\. Using command names as verbs can cause problems for translation\.

    Example:

    **IMPORT **the data\. \(×\)

# Word usage list

## A

### abort, abortion

切勿使用l*abort*。

不要在面向普通受众的内容或用户体验中使用“abort”。如果“abort”出现在无法编辑的 UI 中，请使用替代术语来描述用户的操作。

仅当“abort”是应用程序编程接口（API）的一部分时，才对技术受众使用该术语。在注释和描述中使用替代术语。

替代术语：

- *End*：用于通信和网络连接。

- *Close*：用于应用程序和程序。

- *Stop*：用于硬件操作。

- *Cancel*：用于请求和进程。



### about, approximately

对于CG技术信息，请使用approximately。

### above

不要用来表示更早。

不要用作名词前（上一节）或名词后（上面的代码）的形容词。使用超链接，或使用

hyperlink, 或使用 *previous*, *preceding*, 或 *earlier*\.

### add

一般来说，不要用add来指代软件安装，而要用install。

### allow

避免表述无生命物体赋予人能力，例如“产品允许你……”。只要有可能，就使用直接、以用户为中心的替代表述，如“你可以使用产品来……”或“有了这个产品，用户可以……”。



### a\.m\.

为了方便全球观众，尽可能使用24小时制时间表示法。如果必须使用a\.m\.和p\.m\.，请使用小写字母并加句点。不要使用A\.M\.或P\.M\.。

### among vs\. between

当提及三个或更多的人、事物，或者数量不明确时，使用“among”。

当提及两个人或事物时，或每次提及两个项目之间的关系时（无论项目总数多少），使用“between”。

### and so on

仅在列出清晰的元素序列（如1、2、3等）时使用

或者周一、周二、周三等等。

当列出不构成明确顺序的项目时，请使用适当的描述性措辞，如设备驱动程序、固件和其他代码。

### and/or

可以使用，但前提是你不能单独使用“and”或“or”，或者无法改写句子以避免这种结构。

### antivirus

用作形容词，而非名词。不要加连字符。指旨在检测和响应恶意软件的计算机程序。

### appear

*“display”需要直接宾语；“appear”则不需要。如有必要，你可以使用被动语态“is displayed”。*

### as

不要用来表示“因为”或“当……时”。

### as well as

不要用来表示“和”。

### available

使用“*available*”而非“*active*”来描述已显示且已启用的界面控件。

### B

将此缩写（大写）与乘数前缀结合使用，例如

K、M、G或T表示字节（例如，KB）。

### back up

Write in two words when used as a verb\. Write as *backup *when used as a noun\.

### backward

### because of

在状语从句中使用“because of”，而不是“due to”。

虽然在现代英语用法中，due to作为because of的同义词已被部分接受，但它表意不精确，因此不适合用于技术写作。

Example:

Due to the power failure, the update stopped\. \(×\)

Because of the power failure, the update stopped\. \(√\)

### beep

使用此词代替*alarm*或*tone *，专门指代哔哔声。

Example:

If malfunction occurs during operation, the device beeps or a message appears on the screen\. \(√\)

### blink

### Bluetooth

## C

### can

Use to indicate ability\. Use *could *instead\. In most cases, use *can *or *might, *not *may*\.

### can not

Do not use\. Use *cannot *instead\.

### cancel

Use *cancel *or *stop*, not *abort*\.

### case insensitive

Do not use\. Use *not case sensitive *instead\.

### check

Do not use to refer to marking a check box\. Use *select *instead\.

### choose

Do not use to refer to actions performed on interface elements\. Use *click *or *double\-click *for menu commands, buttons, and other interface elements\. Use *select *and *clear *for check boxes\.

### click

Use *click *, not *click on *to refer to the action of selecting something on a graphical interface\.

For CG technical information, use *select *instead of *click *or *tap*\.

### close

Use *close *for windows, documents, and dialog boxes\. For programs, use *exit*\. For network connections, use *end*\.

### compare to vs\. compare with

Use *compare to *to point out similarities between dissimilar items\. Use *compare with *to comment on the similarities or differences between similar items\. The use of compare to, which is often metaphorical, is generally unnecessary in technical content\.

Example:

People have compared a computer to a human brain\.

Compared with a Pentium 4 processor, a 386 processor is extremely slow\.

### complete

Do not use as a reflexive verb \(for example, *the program completes*\)\. Use *is completed *\(for example, *the program is completed*\)\.

### comprised of

Do not use\. *Comprise *has a history of misuse and is misunderstood even by many native English speakers\. Use *consists of *or *composed of *to describe parts of a whole\.

Example:

The library is composed of 5000 books\.

### criteria

Use only as a plural noun\. For the singular form, use *criterion*\.





## D

### data

Use as a singular noun with a singular verb, even though *data *is the plural of

*datum*\.

### debug

*Debug *is a valid technical term in content for software developers\. Do not use *debug *in any context as a synonym for *troubleshoot*\. Use *troubleshoot *or a more accurate word or phrase instead\.

### desire

Do not use\. Use *want*\.

### desired

Do not use\. Use *required *or *the \<item\> that you want*\.

### dialog

Spell as shown to refer to communication with a computer \- not *dialogue*\. Do not use *dialog *for *dialog box*\.

### dimmed

Do not use\. If an interface control is displayed but is not available, use *disabled*\. If the control is not displayed, use *unavailable*\.

### disable

Do not use to refer to making something ineffective or incapable\. Use only to describe the act of making an interface control or product function unavailable for users of the product\.

### display

Use only as a transitive verb\.

Example:

The message is displayed\. \(√\)

The system displays a message\. \(√\) The message displays\. \(×\)

### done

Do not use *when you are done *\. It’s colloquial\. Use *when you have finished*

instead\.

### double\-click

Write as shown \- hyphenated\. Also, use *double\-click*, not *double\-click on *to describe the action\.

## E

### e\.g\.

Do not use\. Use *for example*\.

### either

Use to mean *any one of two*\.

Although *either *can be used to mean *each*, as in *close the latch on either side of the cover*, avoid this usage\. Use *each *to avoid confusion or imprecision\.

### ensure

Write as shown \- not *insure*, except in the context of an insurance policy\. Use

*ensure *to mean *to make sure *or *to guarantee*\.

### enter

Use to refer to entering text in fields in a graphical interface\. Alternately, use

*type*\.

### etc\.

Do not use\. Use *and so on *when you list a clear sequence of elements\. When you list items that do not form a clear sequence, use appropriate descriptive wording\.

### exit

Use to refer to closing a program\. Do not use to refer to closing a document or a window\. Do not use to refer to switching from one program, document, or window to another\.

## F

### following

Use *following *to introduce art, a table, or, in some cases, a list\.

**for details about vs\. for information on**

For CG technical information, use *for information on *\. For other technical information, use *for details about*\.

## G

### gray

Do not use to refer to commands or options\. Use *unavailable *or *dimmed *instead\. If you need to describe the appearance of check boxes with portions of a larger selection that are already selected, use *shaded*, not *grayed*\.

### ID

user ID

### if vs\. when vs\. whether

To avoid ambiguity, use *if *to express a condition; use *whether *to express uncertainty, and use *when *for situations requiring preparation or to denote the passage of time\.

### in order to

Use *to *in most cases to avoid wordiness\.

## L

### less vs\. fewer vs\. under

Use *less *to refer to a mass amount, value, or degree\. Use *fewer *to refer to a countable number of items\. Do not use *under *to refer to a quantity or number\.

### lower left, lower right

Use *lower left *or *lower right*, not *bottom left *or *bottom right*, to refer to the location of an item in an interface\.



## M

### may

Use only to indicate permission\. Otherwise, use *can *or *might*\.

*May *is overused in technical information and should in most instances be changed to *can *or *might*\.

**mains supply, main power supply**

For CG technical information, use *main power supply*\. For MGI technical information, use *mains supply*\.

### must

Use *must*, not *have to *or *need to*, unless *must *is too restrictive\.

## O

### on\-screen keyboard

A keyboard representation on the screen that the user touches to input characters\.

### over

To avoid ambiguity, use *over *to refer to a position or location above something\. For quantities, use *more than*\. Do not use to refer to version numbers\. List each applicable version instead\.

## P

### please

Do not use in technical information\. Terms of politeness are superfluous, convey the wrong tone for technical material, and are not regarded the same way in all cultures\. In marketing information, terms of politeness might be appropriate\.

### pop\-up

Use only if you must specify the type of a menu, list, or window\.

### preinstalled

Write as shown\.

## R

### real\-time, real time

Write as two words in *in real time*\. Hyphenate as an adjective\.

### recommend

Use *It is recommended that*, not *It is recommended to*\.

### remote

All right to use as an adjective to describe a person or computer at another site\. Do not use *remote *as a verb\.



## T

### toward

Use *toward*, not *towards*\.

### that vs\. which

*That *and *which *are often confusing\. Be sure to use the appropriate word\.

*That *introduces a restrictive clause, which is essential for the sentence to make sense\. A restrictive clause often defines the noun or phrase preceding it and is not separated from it by a comma\.

*Which *introduces a nonrestrictive clause, which is a clause that could be omitted without affecting the meaning of the sentence\. It is preceded by a comma\. Nonrestrictive clauses often contain auxiliary or parenthetical information\.

In general, do include the word *that *in restrictive clauses, even though the sentence may be clear without it\. Including that prevents ambiguity and makes the sentence easier for non\-native English speakers and machine translation to interpret\.

## U

### update vs\. upgrade

Use *update *as a verb to describe the action of installing a software update or service pack\.

Use *upgrade *to refer to a software package that replaces an installed version of a product with a newer version of the same product\. The upgrade process typically leaves existing customer data and preferences intact while replacing the existing software with the newer version\.

Do not use *upgrade *as a synonym for *update*, *service pack*, or any other release that occurs between product versions\.

### uppercase

Use it as one word that functions both as an adjective and as a noun\. When lowercase and uppercase are used together, do not use a suspended hyphen\.

Example:

You can quickly change the capitalization of all uppercase and lowercase letters\. \(√\)

### using vs\. by using

To help the worldwide audience and to reduce the possibility of ambiguity that makes localization more difficult, use *by using *instead of *using *by itself, even if the preposition seems unnecessary\.

However, for CG technical information, *using *is preferred\.
